IN THE FIELD

A Heart as Big as His Milk Jug

What separates Kevin Frantzen from an ordinary milkman isn't just the quality of his whole milk — it's the tenderness he shows to every creature he encounters on his rounds. From injured sparrows to disoriented toads, Denver's wildlife knows it has a friend in Kevin.

Kevin rescuing a disoriented dove · Denver, CO
"He stopped mid-delivery, set down six bottles of whole milk, and spent twenty minutes coaxing a stunned starling back to health. That's just Kevin." — A neighbor on his route

Every morning as Kevin loads his cart and sets out across Denver's neighborhoods, he keeps one eye on the road and one eye on the world around him. Fledgling birds that have tumbled from their nests. Squirrels tangled in garden netting. The occasional dazed rabbit blinking in the pre-dawn quiet. Kevin stops for all of them.

"The milk can wait five minutes," he's been known to say. "This little guy can't."

Friends describe him as a man whose vendetta against The Mad Cow has never once extended to the innocent creatures of Denver. He draws a clear and principled line: one rogue cow stole his bicycle. Every other animal on Earth has earned his protection. He carries a small first-aid kit for wildlife in his milk cart alongside the glass bottles — gauze, a medicine dropper, a soft cloth — just in case.

🗝️ Official City Commendation

The Quail Incident

On a Tuesday morning in June, Kevin spotted a panicked quail darting into traffic on Colorado Boulevard — one of Denver's busiest roads. Without hesitation, he parked the milk truck, stepped into the street, and calmly shepherded the bird to safety through four lanes of rush-hour traffic. Seventeen cars stopped. Two people cried. One man got out and applauded.

Word reached City Hall within the week. Mayor Mike Johnston presented Kevin with the Key to the City of Denver at a small ceremony that Kevin described as "pretty cool, but honestly the quail was the real hero." He kept the key in his milk cart for three weeks before his mother made him bring it inside.

⚔ AN ETERNAL RIVALRY ⚔

Since The Dawn of Milk

A tale of two men. One pure of heart. One absolutely unhinged.

🐄
THE ANCIENT WORLD · ~3000 BC

The First Offense

In the fertile plains of Mesopotamia, a young milkman named Ke-Vin hauled clay jugs of fresh goat milk across the village. His rival — a madman in a poorly stitched cowhide — knocked every single one over. On purpose. While maintaining eye contact. Nobody did anything about it.

⚔️
MEDIEVAL ENGLAND · 1247 AD

The Great Dairy Heist of Shrewsbury

Sir Kevinus of Frantzen, beloved dairy knight, had just completed his morning milk rounds when a masked figure in a black-and-white tabard galloped off with his entire cart. A local monk documented the incident: "The dairyman wept most bitterly, and the thief did moo." The cart was never recovered.

🥛
THE WILD WEST · 1883

High Noon at the Creamery

Sheriff Kevin "Two Jugs" Frantzen had just set up Denver's first milk delivery route when a stranger rode into town — cow-print duster, black eye mask, smelled like trouble and curdled dairy. He didn't steal anything that day. He just stood in the street, stared at Kevin for forty-five minutes, and left. Kevin aged ten years that afternoon.

🚲
LAST TUESDAY · DENVER, CO

The Bicycle. The Note. The Moo.

Kevin's trusty delivery bicycle — his steed, his companion, his vehicle of righteousness — vanished from his backyard in broad daylight. Left in its place: a single handwritten note on a piece of paper torn from a Chick-fil-A bag. It read, simply: "moo." Kevin has not slept well since.
